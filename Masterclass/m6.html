Offer Creation: Simplifying Your Approach

I hope you're not overwhelmed with all the technical aspects or maybe you skipped over those modules because you already understand the basics and just need to dial in your offer and target the right audience for your business or service.

The thing about offer creation is that it’s often overly complicated, especially with all the gurus and influencers like Alex Hormozi saying "The offer is king" and "The offer is everything." The truth is, your offer doesn’t have to be complicated; it just needs to be simple and straightforward.

Yes, some people argue that the more you add to your offer, the more excited people get, but on a psychological level, when we feel like we’re getting more, it often feels like we’re getting our money’s worth. However, we all know that quality is more important than quantity. Sometimes when we’re bombarded with too much, we just see it as unnecessary fluff.

In my experience, the best offers are the simple ones—ones that show you are the company that provides the service better than anyone else in the market. Essentially, you're telling your potential clients: "You can get this service elsewhere, but you won’t get the same experience."

At the end of the day, business is all about experience—good customer service and a great experience win every time. So, when I talk to my clients about creating an offer, I focus on these three things: what you’re offering, who you’re offering it to, and why you're better. And you’re better because you can offer a better customer experience.

For example, let’s say you’re a contracting company. Many homeowners have bad experiences with contractors—poor project management, pricing inconsistencies, and unreliable subcontractors are just a few of the issues they face. Most homeowners are hesitant about home improvement projects because they know it can be a big headache. So, as a contracting company, you can set yourself apart by saying, "We understand this is a huge headache for most people, and we specialize in consistent, high-quality service."

The key is to differentiate yourself from your competitors, not by creating a fantasy offer like "we’ll shine your shoes and give you free whiskey." That’s unrealistic and not sustainable. Instead, focus on solving a pain point your target audience faces.

Key Offer Creation Tips

  • Never Compete on Price: Don’t get caught up in discount wars. I don’t even like offering discounts unless they’re baked into your costs. Account for the cost of your product/service, your workers, and marketing expenses before offering a discount. If your CPM (Cost Per Thousand Impressions) is $7, factor that into your pricing, then offer a discount to make it look attractive.
  • Don't Have a Scarcity Mindset: Let other companies fight over the penny-pinchers. Focus on attracting clients who see the value in your service and are willing to pay for quality.
  • Keep It Simple: Focus on a specific pain point. For instance, with contractors, emphasize the ease and consistency of your service—something that many competitors lack.
  • Focus on Quality: Emphasize how your service solves problems that low-quality providers fail to address.
  • Target the Right Audience: Be specific with your offer. The more specific you get, the better. For example, targeting "first-time homeowners" is much more effective than offering general home improvement services.

Real-Life Offer Examples

Example 1: Local Coffee Shop Offer

Target Audience: New moms

Offer: Free coffee or tea for new mothers to relax and recharge.

Why It Works: The offer targets a very specific audience (new moms) who would appreciate a peaceful space and a caffeine boost. It encourages foot traffic from the local community, and those who visit are likely to return.

Example 2: Pet Grooming Salon Offer

Target Audience: New pet owners

Offer: 10% off your pet’s first grooming service.

Why It Works: This offer targets new pet owners who may be looking for grooming services. The discount encourages them to try your service, and once they experience it, they are likely to become repeat customers.

Example 3: Local Gym Offer

Target Audience: Busy professionals

Offer: Free 7-day trial for busy professionals looking for fitness solutions.

Why It Works: Offering a specialized trial for a particular audience segment (busy professionals) creates a sense of exclusivity and makes the offer feel more personalized. People are more likely to take action when they feel like the offer is designed specifically for them.

Targeting Your Ideal Avatar

The more focused your offer, the better. Let’s say you’re targeting local customers—well, the fact that you both live in the same area already gives you a common connection. Then, consider additional factors like profession, age group, hobbies, or goals. For example, if you're an injury lawyer, your target audience may be people looking to maximize their compensation for medical bills. Having three specific criteria to box in your target market helps you create a more effective, personalized message.

It’s better to have multiple ads with messages tailored to specific avatars than to create one broad message for everyone. The more specific you are, the more likely people are to respond positively to your offer.

Conclusion

That’s offer creation in a nutshell. It’s about focusing on simplicity, targeting the right audience, and offering a better experience than your competitors. The more specific your offer, the better chance you have of connecting with potential clients and getting them to buy into your service.
